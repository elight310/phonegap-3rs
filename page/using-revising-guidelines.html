Using Revising Guidelines Sample

In basic terms, revising deals with making changes in your writing until it says what you want it to say. No writer gets it right the first time. Few writers even get it right the second time. In fact, professional writers almost always carry out many revisions before they are satisfied with their work. Don’t be surprised if you have to do the same. To help you make the best revising moves, follow the guidelines listed below:

  1. Set your writing aside. Let it rest. Get it out of your mind for a while. This will help you see your first draft more clearly when you’re ready to revise.
  2. Carefully review your draft when you’re ready. Read it at least two times: once silently and once aloud. Check the ideas, organization, voice, word choice, and sentence fluency of your writing. It also helps to have at least one other person react to your writing—someone whose opinion you trust.
  3. Look at the big picture. Decide if you’ve effectively developed your focus (thesis). If your thinking about the topic has changed, change your focus.
  4. Look at specific chunks of information. Rewrite any parts that aren’t as clear or effective as you would like them to be. Also cut information that doesn’t support your focus, and add ideas if you feel your readers need more information. You may have to change some parts several times before they say what you want them to say.
  5. Evaluate your opening and closing paragraphs. Make sure that they effectively introduce and wrap up your writing. Quick Guide

REVISING IN ACTION

When you revise a first draft, focus on making improvements to the paper as a whole. You can make these improvements in four basic ways: adding, deleting, moving, or reworking information. Sample

  1. Add information. Add information to your writing:

    to spell out key details for making a point
    to clarify or complete an interesting idea
    to link sentences or paragraphs and improve sentence flow

  2. Delete information. Delete material from your draft:

    if it doesn’t support your focus or thesis
    if it seems redundant or repetitious

  3. Reorder material. Move material in your writing:

    if it does not help to create a clear flow of ideas
    if it seems out of order
    if it would have more impact in a different position

  4. Rework material. Rewrite material in your writing:

    if it seems confusing or unclear
    if it doesn’t maintain the proper voice
    if it is too complex and needs to be simplified

Learn to think like a reader when you revise, and ask yourself these types of questions: Does this passage make sense? Does my essay begin and end effectively? Is this the right voice? Do any sentences sound confusing? Check List

KNOWING YOUR PURPOSE AND AUDIENCE

Always know why you are writing—your purpose. Are you sharing information, arguing for or against something, analyzing a process? When you have a clear purpose, it is much easier to know what to change in your writing. Also consider your readers—your audience. How much do they know about your topic? Lastly, make sure that your writing voice matches the intended audience and purpose.
